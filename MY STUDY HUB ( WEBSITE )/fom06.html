The management functions and Interrelationship of managerial functions

  • Dividing job of managing into its elements for in-depth analysis and understanding.

  • In practice, managers do not neatly divide their job into certain functions

  • If you ask a manager what exactly he does, probably he will read out a jumble of activities which do not easily categorise into a set of functions.

  • Categorisation is the job of management theorists and not practitioners.

  • Different experts, including Fayol, developed their own classification of managerial functions.

  • Generally scholars agree on planning, organising, staffing, directing and controlling as the basic functions of management.

Planning

  • Setting objectives and making policies/ programmes to achieve them.

  • It is a future-oriented activity which includes estimating and forecasting.

Organising

  • Refers to formal grouping of people and activities to achieve objectives

  • Organising involves (1) determining activities, (2) grouping activities (3) assigning groups to a manager, (4) delegation of authority and (5) provision for coordination through authority and information

    • horizontally and vertically in the organisation structure.

Staffing

  • It is the process of determining manpower requirements and recruiting persons.

  • This involves inviting applications, selection, training, etc.

  • It involves fitting the right person to the right job and developing human resources.

Directing

  • Activating plans and group efforts in desired direction

  • Involves implementation of plans and programmes

  • It involves leadership, supervision, communication and motivation

Controlling

  • Checking, measuring and regulating the on-going activities

  • Ensuring desired results

  • Three basic steps in the control process.

    • (i) Establishment of standards: focus on critical variables rather than all variables

    • (ii) Measurement of performance: may be quantitative goals or qualitative

    • (iii) Correcting deviations: to pinpoint the occurrence outside the permissible range of action to allow management to take corrective action.

Nature and significance of staffing

Meaning of Staffing: employing and developing human resources for carrying out the various managerial and non-managerial activities.

Nature of staffing: staffing includes activities such as manpower or human resources planning, recruitment, selection, placement, training and development, remuneration, performance appraisal, promotion, transfers and so on. In many organisations, most of the above activities are handled by the personnel management or human resources management department.

Significance or importance of staffing:

  1. Right manpower: Staffing helps in getting the right people for the right job at the right time. It helps to find out as to how many workers are required and with what qualifications and experience.

  2. Productivity: Staffing contributes to improved productivity. Proper selection brings quality workers, and proper training the performance level of the workers can be improved.

  3. Motivation: It helps in providing job satisfaction and keeping morale high. With proper training and development programmes their efficiency improves and they feel assured of their career advancements.

  4. Harmony: Staffing maintains harmony in the organisation. Through proper staffing, individuals are not just recruited and selected but their performance is regularly appraised and promotions made on merit.
