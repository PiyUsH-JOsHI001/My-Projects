Importance of management

  1. Attainment of Goals: Every organisation has a goal to achieve. It is only through proper management – by well thought of planning, good direction and proper coordination and control that effectiveness to the efforts of each group to achieve given goals can be ensured.

  2. Stability and Growth: Management ensures stability to the working of the organisation and contributes to its growth.

  3. Change and Development : Management keeps itself in touch with the changes in the environment and foresees development in the future. Accordingly, plans are made to keep the organisation ready to meet the challenges.

  4. Efficiency and Effectiveness: By proper planning, staffing, organising, coordinating, directing, and its controlling activities, the management helps in achieving efficiency and effectiveness to human efforts and operations.

Development of management thought

Manage activities with a large number of people must have been known even in the ancient past. There is evidence about the practice of management since early civilizations like construction of the Egyptian Pyramids, or the Great Wall of China.

Major changes took place with the industrial revolution in the 18th century with large scale factories using mechanical power and employing hundreds of people. So the process of management and functions got attention from management experts. Thinking of management as a separate field of learning and practice began in the 19th century.

Various approach of viewpoints: Traditional or classical viewpoint (1880-1920): Scientific, Administrative and Bureaucratic; Behavioural viewpoint (1920-1940): Human Relations and Behavioural Science; Modern viewpoint (1940-) : System, Contingency and Quality

F.W. Taylor’s Scientific management: Mainly focused on increasing productive efficiency of individual workers. The scientific principles: (i) Development of scientific methods in setting work standards and the best way of doing work (ii) Scientific selection and placement of workers best suited to perform the tasks and training and development (iii) Clear division of work and responsibility between management and workers (iv) Harmonious relations and close cooperation with workmen.

Henri Fayol’s Administrative Theory: In Scientific Management, the role of managers and their functions were not given adequate attention. Fayol classified business activities in six areas: (i) Technical: production and maintenance (ii) Commercial: buying, selling and exchange (iii) Financial: Capital (iv) Security: protection of property and human beings (v) Accounting: of stores and equipment (vi) Managerial: planning, organising, commanding, coordinating and control.

Max Weber’s Bureaucratic Theory: The main features (a) Hierarchy of authority: relationship and chain of command (b) Division of work: based on competence (c) Policy, rules, and regulations: to ensure discipline and control (d) Rule of law: for impersonal behaviour based on position in the hierarchy (e) Standardisation of methods, systems, processes (f) Selection and promotion: based on competence (g) Legal power: from official position held by individual.
